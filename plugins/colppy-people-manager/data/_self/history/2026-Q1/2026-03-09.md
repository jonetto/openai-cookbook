# 2026-03-09 — Self-reflection
_Group call with: Alejandro Soto, Malén Baigorria (confrontation session)_

## What I noticed about my own leadership

- **I facilitated a confrontation that was direct but controlled.** I named the problems explicitly — migration delay, information gatekeeping, defensive patterns, standards gap — without personal attack. The line "cada vez que reaccionás así, me vas a tener mucho más alejado" was direct about consequences without being punitive. I'm learning to name patterns in real-time rather than accumulating frustration.

- **I used evidence, not opinion, as the confrontation instrument.** Bringing Malén with structured 1:1 data from every engineer made the conversation undeniable. Ale's reflex — "a mí nadie me lo levantó" — couldn't hold against independent, patterned evidence from every team member. This is a better approach than my instinct, which is to argue from my own observations. The evidence-based confrontation + co-facilitator model worked and should be standard for future high-stakes leadership conversations.

- **I drew a hard line and held it.** "No existen más planes de seis meses. Sacátelo de la cabeza." This was necessary and overdue. I'd been saying softer versions of this in group meetings for weeks, and Ale deflected every time. The format mattered: private, with data, with a People partner present. The lesson is that group-meeting challenges allow defense; private confrontation with data doesn't.

- **I stopped the intensity instinct.** When Ale proposed "12 hours in a room" for the team, I redirected toward structured, regular amplification of knowledge. This is a growth signal for me — my own instinct has historically been similar (marathon sessions, pressure until resolution). Recognizing and stopping it in someone else suggests I'm developing the pattern recognition to stop it in myself.

- **Airtime distribution was intentional.** Malén opened with People data. I layered business expectations. We didn't overlap or contradict. The pre-alignment (exit decision on March 6) gave us a unified frame. I let Malén make the interpersonal observations ("sos un pasajero") that would have landed differently from me. This is delegation of coaching authority — a development area for me.

- **The escalation threshold problem.** I was raising speed/value concerns every Monday and Friday in group meetings where Ale denied delays. I framed this as Ale not hearing the feedback. But the real gap was mine: I was using the wrong format. Group meetings are not the right venue for confronting a leader whose first instinct is defense. I should have escalated from group challenge to individual confrontation earlier — probably 3-4 weeks earlier. The delay cost time and allowed the team damage to deepen. Next time: if a leader deflects the same feedback twice in a group setting, move to a private confrontation within the week, not after weeks of repeated group attempts.

- **I held the "your responsibility" frame.** Multiple times, I redirected from "Jorge's problem" or "the team's problem" to "your responsibility as area leader." This is the right frame, but I noticed I had to reassert it repeatedly because Ale's instinct is to externalize. The repetition is necessary — the question is whether it lands or just creates compliance.

## Connection to my development areas

### Delegation and trust
The exit decision (March 6) represents a boundary on delegation: I've concluded this person can't carry the delegation I need. But the managed transition requires a different kind of delegation — trusting Malén to co-facilitate, trusting the process to hold, trusting that the commitments extracted (even under pressure) serve organizational purposes during transition. I'm not withdrawing trust impulsively; I'm making a calibrated withdrawal based on accumulated evidence. That's different from my historical pattern of tightening control when disappointed.

### Emotional impact vs. intent
The confrontation was intense but calibrated. I was direct ("no existen más planes de seis meses") without being cruel. I named consequences ("cada vez que reaccionás así, me vas a tener mucho más alejado") without ultimatums. I think the emotional impact matched the intent this time. But I should check with Malén in our next 1:1 — she observed the dynamic and can tell me if my directness crossed from productive to damaging at any point.

### Talent development
This session exposed the cost of delayed escalation. The team is at retention risk — Federico immediate, Mario by late May, Ignacio medium-term. Some of this damage accumulated while I was raising concerns in the wrong format (group meetings). The talent development failure is not just Ale's — it's mine. I didn't intervene at the right layer (individual confrontation) soon enough. The new Tech Lead's first act needs to signal openness to technical debate, especially for Mario and Federico.

### Team culture vs. accountability
The confrontation created accountability (three commitments) but not culture change. Culture change requires sustained conditions — not a single high-pressure meeting. The risk: Ale complies with the commitments in a transactional way (check the box) without internalizing the feedback. Given the exit decision, this is acceptable for transition purposes. But for future leaders, I need to build accountability structures that create cultural conditions, not just compliance.

### Strategic vs. operational presence
I went deep into operations in this meeting — discussing specific migration timelines, Jorge's 1:1 cadence, individual engineer feedback. This was necessary for this conversation. But it's the kind of session that pulls me down from the balcony. After the transition, I need to ensure the new Tech Lead gets setup that prevents me from needing to do this again. The goal: never be in a room where I know more about an engineer's 1:1 schedule than their leader does.
